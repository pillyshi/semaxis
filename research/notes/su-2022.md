## Why It Matters For SemAxis

Su et al. are the primary source for Vote-K, which is already one of SemAxis's
sample selection methods. The paper frames example selection as a two-stage
process: first choose a small, diverse, representative pool from unlabeled data
to annotate; then retrieve task examples from that pool at test time.

SemAxis does not annotate selected examples for in-context prediction. It uses
selected texts during `fit` to prompt an LLM to generate a hypothesis bank.
Even so, the selection objective transfers: a small prompt budget should expose
the LLM to examples that cover the dataset rather than a random or redundant
slice.

This paper therefore supports retaining Vote-K as a strong baseline, but not as
an unqualified default. The paper itself shows that Vote-K's value depends on a
second-stage similarity retrieval setup; SemAxis's one-stage fit-time sampling
should evaluate Vote-K against random, K-Means, and future coverage-based
alternatives under fixed prompt budgets.

## Method

The proposed framework has two steps:

1. Selective annotation: choose a small subset `L` from unlabeled examples
   before test time.
2. Prompt retrieval: retrieve in-context examples from `L` for each test input.

Vote-K is an unsupervised graph-based selective annotation method. The paper
embeds unlabeled examples with Sentence-BERT, builds a directed k-nearest
neighbor graph, and iteratively selects examples with high incoming support
while discounting examples close to already selected points. This encourages
representativeness and diversity.

The full algorithm then uses an LLM confidence score to bucket remaining
examples and selects additional high-graph-score examples across confidence
buckets. The reported default search found `k = 150` and `rho = 10` useful
across many datasets.

At prompt time, examples are retrieved by embedding similarity from the selected
annotated pool. The paper orders in-context examples by ascending similarity so
the most similar example appears later in the prompt, exploiting recency bias.

## Evaluation

- Datasets: MRPC, SST-5, DBpedia, MNLI, RTE, HellaSwag, MultiWOZ, GeoQuery,
  Natural Questions, and XSum.
- Tasks: classification, multiple-choice commonsense reasoning, dialogue state
  tracking, semantic parsing, open-domain QA, and summarization.
- Models: GPT-J and Codex variants by default, with analyses using GPT-Neo,
  GPT-3, and OPT-175B.
- Annotation budgets: especially `18` and `100` examples.
- Stability: repeat subsampling of `3K` unlabeled instances to simulate
  randomness in collecting unlabeled data.

The headline result is that Vote-K outperforms random selection across the 10
datasets. The paper reports average relative gains of `12.9%` with an
annotation budget of `18`, and `11.4%` with a budget of `100`, compared with
random annotation. In the detailed main table, Vote-K improves every listed
task for both budgets.

The paper also reports that Vote-K reduces variance across unlabeled-subsample
trials and improves diversity and representativeness measures relative to
random and K-Means in their analysis.

## Limitations Relevant To SemAxis

Vote-K was designed for selective annotation followed by per-test prompt
retrieval. SemAxis's use is different: selected examples are shown once during
training-time hypothesis generation. That means SemAxis should not import
Vote-K's reported gains as direct evidence that Vote-K will generate better
hypotheses.

The paper itself shows that Vote-K can perform poorly when the second-stage
retrieval is random rather than similarity-based. Its appendix reports low
average similarity between context examples and inputs when Vote-K selection is
paired with random retrieval, and explains poor performance in that setting.
For SemAxis, this means selected-example order, retrieval context, and prompt
construction are part of the method.

There is also a potential outlier issue. The paper argues Vote-K improves both
diversity and representativeness, but later work cited in the survey notes that
Vote-K-style diversity can still overselect outliers in some settings. SemAxis
should benchmark it rather than promote it as the only recommended strategy.

## Comparison With Current SemAxis

| Dimension | Su et al. | SemAxis Today |
|---|---|---|
| Selection purpose | Choose examples to annotate for ICL | Choose texts to prompt hypothesis generation |
| Selection method | Vote-K graph selection plus confidence buckets | Random, K-Means, or Vote-K |
| Retrieval stage | Similarity retrieval per test input | No per-test retrieval for generation |
| Embedding model | Sentence-BERT/all-mpnet-base-v2 | Config-dependent embeddings |
| Evaluation focus | Few-shot ICL accuracy under annotation budget | Hypothesis quality, feature matrix utility, cost |
| Logging need | Selected pool, retrieval examples, prompt order | Selected texts, sample method, embedding config, prompt order |

## Actionable Findings

1. Cite Su et al. as the Vote-K source for SemAxis sampling.
2. Add a sampling benchmark candidate rather than treating Vote-K as settled:
   compare random, K-Means, Vote-K, and coverage-based strategies under equal
   token budgets.
3. Record selected example ids, graph parameters such as `k` and `rho` when
   applicable, embedding model, similarity metric, and prompt order in run
   artifacts.
4. Evaluate not only predictive performance but also generated-hypothesis
   redundancy, label-proxy rate, stability across seeds, and NLI scoring cost.
5. Be explicit that SemAxis uses Vote-K outside the original selective
   annotation plus retrieval setting.
